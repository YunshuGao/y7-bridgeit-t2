Five bridge types — and our first mini-build

Week 1 · 75 minutes · Year 7 Technology (Mandatory) · Bridge It

TE4-8ENTE4-10TS

🎯 Learning intentions

  • I can name and sketch the 5 main bridge types.
  • I can explain which type is best for a short span vs a long span.
  • I can build a simple index-card bridge that spans 20 cm and holds a pencil case.

✅ Success criteria

  • My folio has labelled sketches of all 5 bridge types.
  • I have built and tested an index-card bridge with my partner.
  • I can explain (verbally) why a suspension bridge needs cables and towers.

Do Now · 5 min

Quick recall from Lesson 1 — without looking, match the bridge to the country:

  • Akashi-Kaikyō · Millau · Sydney Harbour · Golden Gate
  • France · USA · Japan · Australia

📝 Folio zone

My four matches: ________________________

I Do · ~10 min Teacher demonstration

Teacher models the 5 bridge types with a sketch on the board. You copy into your folio.

  1. Beam bridge — horizontal beam across supports. Simplest. Short spans only (<80 m). Example: concrete highway overpass.
  2. Arch bridge — curved arch pushes load outward into abutments. Can hold huge loads. Example: Sydney Harbour Bridge (also has truss!).
  3. Truss bridge — triangular framework distributes load through all members. The type WE will build. Example: old steel rail bridges.
  4. Suspension bridge — deck hangs from cables draped over tall towers. Huge spans (>1 km). Example: Golden Gate.
  5. Cable-stayed bridge — cables run directly from tower to deck (no drape). Example: Anzac Bridge in Sydney.
Key rule: longer span = more complex bridge type needed. Beam can do 80 m. Truss can do 150 m. Cable-stayed can do 1 km. Suspension can do 2 km.

We Do · ~15 min Guided practice

Class force-path game. Ms Gao draws each bridge and asks: "When a truck drives across, where does the force go?"

  • Beam → force bends the beam down → compression on top, tension on bottom
  • Arch → force pushes sideways into the abutments
  • Truss → force zigzags through every triangle
  • Suspension → force pulls down → cables pull up → towers push down into ground
  • Cable-stayed → direct cable pull → tower compression → ground

Together we label one sketch with red arrows (compression) and blue arrows (tension).

You Do · ~35 min Independent pair work

Mini-build #1 — Index Card Bridge (30 min)

With your partner: using ONLY 3 index cards + 30 cm masking tape, build a bridge that spans a 20 cm gap between two desks and holds a pencil case for 10 seconds.

  1. Plan — 3 min. Sketch your design in your folio before building.
  2. Build — 20 min.
  3. Test — hang your pencil case on the deck. Did it hold?
  4. Reflect — what bridge TYPE did you just build? Was it beam, arch, truss? Write 2 sentences.
WHS: scissors out only for card-cutting. Pass handle-first. Cut away from body.

Differentiation

🪜Support

Provided pre-cut card strips and a photo of a beam-and-brace example. Pair with a Core student.

🎯Core

Build the index card bridge with no template — use your own design.

🚀Extension

After success, try to span 30 cm with the same 3 cards + tape. What had to change?

Exit Ticket · 5 min

Write in your folio — 3 sentences:

  1. Name the 5 bridge types.
  2. Which type would YOU build for a 500 m gap? Why?
  3. What bridge type did your index-card bridge act like today?
